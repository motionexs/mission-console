How an Athlete May Perceive Injury
⚡ Disastrous
Catastrophic setback to athletic identity and career trajectory.
🏅 Opportunity for Courage
A chance to demonstrate mental toughness and resilience.
🛡 Excuse for Poor Performance

🚪 Escape from a Losing Team
Relief from a toxic team culture or underperforming environment.
👨‍👩‍👦 Escape from a Domineering Parent

Predictors of Injury & Interventions
Injury-Prone Personality Characteristics
⚠ No single personality type is universally injury-prone. However, common traits include:
Risk Takers Reserved / Detached Tender-Minded Apprehensive Over-protective Easily Distracted
These athletes lack the ability to cope with stress associated with risk and its consequences.
Contributing Mechanisms
Emotional Stress → Injury
Emotionally stressed athletes are MORE prone to injury than emotionally adjusted athletes.
Aggression as Anxiety Outlet
Angry athletes may take frustration out on others, losing perspective on safe conduct.
Fear of Failure Cycle
Continuing to play through injury to avoid failing perpetuates the injury cycle.
Skill & Coordination Loss
Emotional distress impairs technique and motor control, creating avoidable risk.
Stress & Risk of Injury

Positive stressors (e.g. making all-conference) are also present but less likely to cause injury.
INTERVENTION STRATEGY
Athletes with declining performance and personality changes need a less demanding training program. Confer with the athlete to reveal hidden emotional/physical problems. Refer to a counselor, psychologist, or physician as appropriate.
● OBJECTIVE 4
4
Stressors in the Athlete's Life
⚡ Sport-Specific Stressors
  • Sports are inherently stressful environments
  • Fine line between peak performance and overtraining
  • Unreasonable expectations from athletes themselves
  • Unreasonable expectations from coaches
  • Unreasonable expectations from parents
  • Peripheral stressors imposed on the athlete from the sporting environment
🏠 Life Stressors (Peripheral)
  • School and academic pressure
  • Work obligations outside of sport
  • Family conflicts and relationship difficulties
  • Financial concerns
  • Can be a major cause of emotional stress
Overtraining Continuum
OVERTRAINING
  • Physical load > coping capacity
  • Physiological + psychological
  • Anxiety: fear, apprehension, restlessness
  • Heart palpitations, shortness of breath
  • Sweaty palms, headaches, constriction in throat
  • Children may fail purposely to escape pressure
→
STALENESS
  • Deteriorating performance standards
  • Chronic fatigue and apathy
  • Loss of appetite, indigestion, weight loss
  • Inability to sleep or rest
  • ↑ Risk for acute and overuse injuries
  • ↑ Risk for infections
  • Emotional: worries, fears, anxiety
→
BURNOUT
  • Physical and emotional exhaustion
  • Negative self-concept
  • Negative sports attitudes
  • Loss of concern for feelings of others
  • Frequent headaches, GI disturbances
  • Sleeplessness, chronic fatigue
  • Depersonalization, cynicism
  • ↓ Sense of accomplishment, depressed mood
Overtraining Recovery Protocol
Recognize early → short interruption 3–5 days → maintain same intensity, reduce volume → gradually return to previous workload → remove from competition during recovery period.
● OBJECTIVE 5

Buffers for Stress Management
Buffers are protective mechanisms incorporated within the goal-setting and rehabilitation paradigm that reduce the psychological impact of stress on the injured athlete.
🏆
Positive Reinforcement
Reward and recognition when goals are met during rehabilitation — builds motivation and compliance.
🕒
Time Management
Structure for incorporating rehabilitation goals into a daily lifestyle — reduces feeling of chaos and loss of control.
🤝
Social Support
Setting goals jointly with the coach or fitness professional creates a feeling of social support and belonging.
💪
Self-Efficacy
Feelings of increased self-efficacy when rehabilitation goals are achieved — builds confidence for return.
Additional Stress Management Techniques (used during rehabilitation)
Relaxation Imagery Cognitive Restructuring Thought Stopping
Athlete has a more successful rehabilitation when fully engaged in the activity of rehabilitation. These techniques lessen the stressful reaction to injury. See Objective 11 for full detail.
● OBJECTIVE 6
6
Goal Setting as a Motivator to Rehabilitation Compliance
Goal setting is an effective motivator for compliance to rehabilitation. Goals must be personal, internally satisfying, and set as a joint venture between athlete and coach/fitness professional.
Nine Factors for Effective Goal Setting
1
Set Specific and Measurable Goals
Goals must be clearly defined and quantifiable — not vague aspirations.
2
Use Positive vs. Negative Language
Frame goals in terms of what the athlete will do, not what to avoid.
3
Challenging but Realistic
Goals should stretch the athlete but remain achievable within their current capacity.
4
Set a Reasonable Timetable
Attach target dates to each goal to create accountability and urgency.
5
Integrate Short-, Medium-, and Long-Term Goals
Layer goals across timescales: daily → weekly → monthly → seasonal/yearly.
6
Link Outcome to Process
Connect the big picture outcome (return to play) to daily process actions.
7
Internalize Goals
Goals must be personally meaningful — not externally imposed by others.
8
Monitor and Evaluate Goals
Regularly review progress; adjust if goals are too easy or too hard.
9
Link Sports Goals to Life Goals
Connect rehabilitation goals to the athlete's broader life aspirations and values.
Goal Timeframes
Daily Goals
Provide a sense of accomplishment — small wins maintain motivation during long rehab.
Weekly Goals
Provide a sense of progress — benchmarks show movement in the right direction.
Monthly / Yearly Goals
Provide long-term achievement milestones — the light at the end of the tunnel.
Key Principles
  • Goal setting is effective in both sport performance AND rehabilitation
  • Goals must be personal and internally satisfying — not imposed on the athlete
  • Setting goals is a joint venture between athlete and coach/fitness professional
  • Long-term goals are achieved through many short-term goals met first
  • Athletes should be aware of the goal and believe it can be met
Buffers Incorporated within the Goal-Setting Paradigm
✓ Positive Reinforcement when goals are met ✓ Time Management into lifestyle ✓ Social Support from joint goal-setting ✓ Increased Self-Efficacy from achievement
● OBJECTIVE 7
7
Considerations in Managing the Psychological Impact of Injury
The sports medicine team, coach, or fitness professional can have a significant impact on how an athlete deals with injury psychologically. Successful communication is critical to recovery time and effort.
BOX 12–2: Eight Things a Coach / Fitness Professional / Athletic Trainer Can Do
1. Be a Good Listener
Listen beyond the complaining. Pay attention to fear, anger, depression, or anxiety in the athlete's voice. Use "I see", "Go ahead", or silence to let them fully express themselves.
2. Be Aware of Body Language
Look athletes in the eye with genuine interest. Body language projects care and respect — athletes notice non-verbal signals as much as spoken words.
3. Project a Caring Image
Consider the athlete as an individual, not just "the sprained ankle." Treat them as an equal. Neglecting injured athletes gives the perception that they are "outcasts."
4. Find Out Exactly What the Problem Is
Allow the athlete to provide as much input as possible about their injury. Identify the problem clearly before offering solutions or reassurance.
5. Explain the Injury to the Athlete
Athletic trainer or physician must clearly explain the injury and its circumstances. Provide a clear, simple explanation the athlete can understand — reduces fear of the unknown.
6. Manage the Stress of Injury
Use techniques to lessen the stressful reaction: Relaxation, Imagery, Cognitive Restructuring, Thought Stopping. Full engagement in rehab activities improves outcomes.
7. Keep the Athlete Involved with the Team
Prevent alienation — especially in long-term rehab. Plan sport-specific drills during practice time. Athlete re-enters team culture, avoids isolation, maintains identity and motivation.
8. Help the Athlete Return to Play
Assist decisions based on facts, not emotions. Address perceptions: is the athlete being held back unfairly, or forced to return too soon? Navigate these tensions carefully.
● OBJECTIVE 8
8
Decision-Making Process for Return to Competition
Who Decides?
The athletic trainer or team physician should decide — not the coach or athlete alone.
Coaches are likely to return players based on status and game situation.
Athletic trainers make decisions based on status of the athlete's injury.
Playing with Pain — Key Considerations
⚠ Severe injury is made worse
Continuing to compete on a serious injury exacerbates the damage and extends total recovery time.
⚠ Long-term consequences
An athlete who plays through unhealed injury constantly reduces chances of a healthy life of activity.
⚠ Fear of losing starting position
Athletes fearing bench time may push for return prematurely — candidates for reinjury and may reinforce a coach's decision to play someone else.
Objective Return Decision Framework
Process
1
Collect baseline performance data in the preseason
2
When athlete appears physically/psychologically ready, retest using same measures
3
Compare scores to preseason baseline
4
Consider physical, emotional, psychological aspects of readiness
Holistic Factors Influencing Return
  • Impact of the environment
  • Support of the athletic community
  • Culture in which the athlete resides at time of injury
  • Rehabilitation is more than just physical, emotional, or psychological — all combine to influence the course from injury through to return

Why Social Support Is Important to the Injured Athlete
Core Principle
  • Athletes should be shown the coach/professional cares for them as a person, not just as a team asset
  • The athlete's perception of the coach or fitness professional makes a difference in recovery time and effort
  • Successful communication is critical — the relationship drives compliance
  • During frustration, showing concern wins athlete's loyalty and dedication
  • Neglecting injured athletes gives them the perception they are "outcasts"
Keeping the Athlete Involved
  • Athletes may feel alienated from the team — critical in long-term rehab
  • Social support prevents feelings of negative self-worth and loss of identity
  • Plan sport-specific drills during practice time with the team
  • Athlete re-enters team culture and is not isolated from team environment
  • Athlete can put more effort into functional, sport-specific situations that are less boring
How to Provide Social Support — Step by Step
Identify the Problem
Allow the athlete to provide as much input about their injury as possible.
Active Listening
Listen beyond the complaining. Attend to fear, anger, depression, or anxiety in the athlete's voice.
Establish Rapport
Show genuine concern; establish a sense of caring for the athlete as an individual.
Explain the Injury
Clear, simple explanation from the athletic trainer or physician reduces fear of the unknown.
Return to Play Assistance
Help the athlete make decisions based on facts — not clouded by emotion or fear.

Differentiating: Counsellor vs. Psychologist vs. Psychiatrist
Coaches and fitness professionals must have appropriate counseling skills to confront an athlete's fears, frustrations, and daily crises — and to refer to the correct professional when serious issues arise.
Professional Qualifications Role / Scope When to Refer
Counsellor
Talk-based		 Degree in counseling, social work, or related field. May have certification in sports counseling. Not typically licensed to diagnose. Addresses daily stressors, performance anxiety, adjustment to injury, goal setting, and coping strategies through structured conversation. Athlete experiencing performance anxiety, adjustment difficulties, general stress, fears, and daily crises that do not constitute clinical disorders.
Psychologist
Diagnosis + therapy		 Doctoral degree (PhD, PsyD, or EdD in psychology). Licensed to practice. Can conduct psychological testing and assessment. Diagnoses and treats mental disorders using psychotherapy. May specialize in sport psychology. Cannot prescribe medication in most jurisdictions. Serious emotional instabilities must be referred to a professional psychologist. Mental disorders, mood disorders, anxiety disorders, identity crises.
Psychiatrist
Medical + medication		 Medical doctor (MD or DO) with specialization in psychiatry. Can prescribe medication. Highest level of clinical training. Diagnoses and treats mental disorders using both psychotherapy AND medication. Handles severe or complex psychiatric conditions requiring pharmacological management. Severe depression, psychosis, bipolar disorder, PTSD requiring medication. When psychotherapy alone is insufficient. Personality disorders requiring antipsychotic medications.
Coach / Fitness Professional's Role in Referral
Coaches and fitness professionals are NOT therapists. Their role is to: (1) recognize signs of emotional disturbance, (2) provide basic counseling support for daily crises, and (3) refer individuals with serious emotional problems to the appropriate professional. Knowing when to refer is as important as the referral itself.
● OBJECTIVE 11
11
Psychological Skills Training Techniques for Managing Injury
Athletic trainers can help injured athletes respond positively to injury via specific psychological training techniques. Serious emotional instabilities must be referred to a professional psychologist — these techniques are for managing, not treating, psychological responses.
🧘

Progressive Relaxation

Most extensively used technique

Involves intense training in the awareness of tension and tension's release. Reduces tension and anxiety systematically through the body.

Protocol
  • Practiced in a reclining position or seated in a chair
  • Each muscle group tensed for 5–7 seconds
  • Then relaxed for 20–30 seconds
  • Moves systematically through all major muscle groups
  • Goal: athlete learns to recognize and release tension consciously
🧠

Meditation

Mental focus technique

Reduces tension and anxiety through sustained mental focus. Requires a quiet environment and intentional concentration.

Methods
  • Focusing on a constant mental stimulus — a phrase, a sound, or a single word repeated silently or audibly
  • Gazing steadily at a fixed object to anchor attention
  • Goal: quieting the mind, reducing anxiety and emotional reactivity
👁

Imagery (Mental Rehearsal)

Using the senses to create or recreate an experience in the mind. Athletes visually rehearse challenges anticipated on return.

  • Rehearse problems that may stand in the way of return to competition
  • Rehearse how they will overcome these problems
  • Builds confidence and motivational skills needed for successful return
  • Can be used alongside relaxation techniques
🔄

Cognitive Restructuring

Changing the way an athlete thinks about their injury — replacing negative, irrational thoughts with more positive, realistic ones.

  • Identify and challenge irrational beliefs about the injury
  • Replace negative self-talk with constructive reframing
  • Addresses thoughts like "I'll never be the same athlete again"
  • Promotes a more realistic, positive cognitive appraisal
🛑

Thought Stopping

Consciously interrupting and halting negative thought patterns before they spiral and negatively impact rehabilitation.

  • Athlete mentally says "STOP" when a negative thought arises
  • Immediately replace the negative thought with a positive one
  • Can be paired with a physical cue (e.g. snapping a rubber band)
  • Prevents negative spirals that impede compliance and motivation

Mental Disorders — Recognition, Referral & Treatment
Mental Health Defined
Includes an individual's psychological, emotional, and social well-being. Defined as a positive mental state that allows an individual to thrive and be resilient. Common in our society.
Mental Disorder Defined
Syndrome characterized by clinically significant disturbances in cognition, emotional regulation, or behavior. Reflects a dysfunction in the psychological, biological, or developmental process underlying mental functioning.
Three Classifications of Mental Disorders
M
Mood Disorders
Characterized by mood swings ranging from happiness to sadness which disrupt normal behavior. Often accompanied by physical symptoms such as sleep or appetite disturbances.

Depression

  • Helplessness and misery
  • Loss of energy
  • Excessive guilt
  • Diminished ability to think or concentrate
  • Changes in eating and sleeping habits
  • Recurrent thoughts of hurting themselves, suicide, or death

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)

  • Mental depression related to a specific season (usually winter)
  • Caused by decrease in sunlight during winter months
  • Symptoms: fatigue, diminished concentration, daytime drowsiness
  • Also: sweating, increased HR, increased BP, stomach discomfort, chills, dry mouth, irritability, sleep disturbances
  • Abnormal when it interferes with emotional well-being or daily functioning
A
Anxiety Disorders
Anxiety is one of the most common mental and emotional stress producers. Reflected by vague fear, apprehension, and restlessness. Athlete may feel inadequate without being able to say why.
Physical symptoms: heart palpitations, shortness of breath, sweaty palms, constriction in the throat, headaches.

Panic Attack

  • Unexpected and unprovoked, intensely fearful experience of terror
  • Occurs in approximately 30% of young adults
  • Most likely to occur at night
  • Tends to run in families (genetic component)
  • Emotionally intense; may feel like a medical emergency

Phobia

  • Persistent and irrational fear of a specific situation, activity, or object
  • Creates intense desire to avoid the feared stimulus
  • Symptoms: increased HR, difficulty breathing, sweating, dizziness
  • Treatments: behavioral modification AND antidepressant or anti-anxiety medications
P
Personality Disorders
Pathological disturbance in cognition, affect, interpersonal functioning, or impulse control. Usually of long duration — can be traced to events or circumstances in adolescence.
Treatment: psychotherapy and/or antipsychotic medications.

Paranoia

Having unrealistic and unfounded suspicions about specific people or things. Persistent distrust without factual basis.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Engaging in unreasonable repetitive acts (e.g. washing hands repeatedly) in response to obsessive thoughts.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Occurs following a psychologically traumatic event — personal assault/abuse, sexual assault or harassment, or a catastrophic event (e.g. plane crash).
