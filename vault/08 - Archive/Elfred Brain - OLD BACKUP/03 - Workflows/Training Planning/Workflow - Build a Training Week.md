## Purpose

This workflow helps me build a simple, logical training week for an athlete.

The goal is to organize training so that the athlete improves without creating unnecessary fatigue, poor movement quality, or injury risk.

## When To Use This

Use this workflow when I need to plan a training week for:

- high jump
- long jump
- speed/power athletes
- general preparation
- specific preparation
- competition preparation
- return to training after fatigue or pain

## Inputs Needed

Before building the week, collect:

- Athlete goal:
- Sport/event:
- Phase of season:
- Training age:
- Fatigue level:
- Injury/pain status:
- Available training days:
- Equipment available:
- Competition date:
- Main technical focus:
- Main physical focus:

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify the phase of season

Ask:

What phase is the athlete in?

Examples:

- general preparation
- specific preparation
- pre-competition
- competition phase
- transition phase
- return-to-training phase

The phase tells me what the main focus should be.

### Step 2: Choose the main goal of the week

Do not try to improve everything at once.

Choose 1–2 main goals.

Examples:

- improve approach rhythm
- build general strength
- improve power
- improve jump technique
- manage fatigue
- return gradually after pain
- prepare for competition

### Step 3: Check athlete readiness

Check:

- fatigue
- soreness
- pain
- sleep
- mood
- motivation
- technical quality
- recent training load

If readiness is poor, adjust the plan before adding more load.

### Step 4: Place the most important sessions first

Put high-quality sessions when the athlete is freshest.

High-quality sessions may include:

- jumps
- sprinting
- power work
- technical sessions
- heavy strength work

Avoid placing too many high-intensity days back-to-back.

### Step 5: Balance stress and recovery

Plan a weekly rhythm.

Example:

- high day
- lower day
- medium day
- recovery day
- high day
- low day
- rest day

The goal is not to train hard every day.

The goal is to train hard when it matters and recover enough to adapt.

### Step 6: Match exercises to the goal

Every session should have a reason.

Ask:

Why is this drill or exercise here?

If it does not support the main goal, remove it.

### Step 7: Set volume and intensity

Decide:

- how many jumps
- how many sprints
- how many sets and reps
- how heavy
- how much rest
- how technically demanding

Start simple and progress slowly.

### Step 8: Add monitoring points

Decide what to watch during the week.

Examples:

- movement quality
- fatigue
- soreness
- jump rhythm
- sprint sharpness
- pain response
- motivation

### Step 9: Review after the week

After the week, ask:

- What worked?
- What did not work?
- Did the athlete improve?
- Was the load too high?
- Was the load too low?
- What should change next week?

## Decision Rules

- If competition is far away, then the week can include more general development.
- If competition is close, then the week should be more specific and less fatiguing.
- If fatigue is high, then reduce volume or intensity.
- If pain is present, then avoid high-risk work and use safer alternatives.
- If technical quality drops, then stop or change the technical work.
- If the athlete is adapting well, then progress slowly.
- If the athlete is struggling, then hold or reduce load before progressing.

## Output

This workflow should produce:

- a weekly training plan
- main weekly goal
- session goals
- training days
- recovery days
- monitoring points
- review notes

## Mistakes To Avoid

- Trying to train everything in one week.
- Placing too many hard days together.
- Ignoring fatigue and pain.
- Copying another athlete’s plan.
- Adding exercises without a reason.
- Increasing volume too quickly.
- Forgetting to review the week afterward.
