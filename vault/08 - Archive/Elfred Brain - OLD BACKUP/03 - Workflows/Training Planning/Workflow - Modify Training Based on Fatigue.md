## Purpose

This workflow helps me decide how to adjust a session when an athlete looks tired, feels tired, or performs poorly.

The goal is to protect movement quality while still getting useful work done.

## When To Use This

Use this workflow when:

- the athlete reports tiredness
- movement quality is poor
- technical rhythm is getting worse
- soreness is higher than normal
- motivation is low
- jump height, sprint speed, or power looks reduced
- the athlete had poor sleep or high life stress
- the planned session feels too much for the athlete on the day

## Inputs Needed

Before adjusting, check:

- Fatigue level:
- Sleep quality:
- Soreness:
- Pain/injury status:
- Mood/motivation:
- Movement quality:
- Previous session load:
- Today’s planned session:
- Competition timeline:

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify the type of fatigue

Ask whether fatigue seems mostly:

- physical
- mental
- emotional
- technical
- accumulated from previous training
- caused by poor sleep or life stress

This matters because not all fatigue needs the same response.

### Step 2: Check for pain

Ask:

Is there pain?

If yes, check:

- where is the pain?
- is it sharp or mild?
- is it getting worse?
- does it change movement?
- did it appear suddenly?
- does it continue after warming up?

Pain changes the decision.

### Step 3: Check movement quality

Look for:

- poor rhythm
- slower approach
- weak takeoff
- poor posture
- heavy landings
- poor coordination
- lack of sharpness
- repeated technical mistakes

Movement quality is often more important than what the plan says.

### Step 4: Choose an adjustment option

Possible options:

1. Continue as planned  
2. Reduce volume  
3. Reduce intensity  
4. Change to technical rhythm work  
5. Change to recovery work  
6. Remove high-impact work  
7. End the session early  
8. Refer or seek medical input if concerning pain is present  

### Step 5: Keep the session useful

Do not think adjustment means failure.

A modified session can still improve:

- rhythm
- mobility
- coordination
- low-intensity technique
- recovery
- confidence
- consistency

### Step 6: Record what happened

Create a short review note.

Include:

- what fatigue signs appeared
- what was changed
- how the athlete responded
- what should change next session

## Decision Rules

- If fatigue is low and movement quality is good, then continue as planned.
- If fatigue is moderate but technique is stable, then reduce volume slightly and monitor.
- If fatigue is high and technique is poor, then change to lower-risk technical or recovery work.
- If pain is present and movement changes, then stop high-risk work.
- If fatigue appears repeatedly, then review the weekly training load.
- If the athlete improves after reducing volume, then the original load may have been too high for that day.
- If the athlete feels tired but moves well, then continue carefully with more rest between efforts.

## Output

This workflow should produce:

- a modified session decision
- reason for adjustment
- safer training option
- monitoring notes
- follow-up action for the next session

## Mistakes To Avoid

- Forcing the original session no matter what.
- Ignoring technical quality.
- Treating all tiredness as laziness.
- Confusing pain with normal training discomfort.
- Reducing everything too quickly without observing properly.
- Not writing down the lesson afterward.
- Making emotional decisions instead of structured decisions.
