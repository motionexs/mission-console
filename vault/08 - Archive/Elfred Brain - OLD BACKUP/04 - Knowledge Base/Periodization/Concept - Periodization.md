## Simple Definition

Periodization is the planned organization of training over time.

It answers the question:

How do we plan training so the athlete improves and performs well at the right time?

## Technical Definition

Periodization is the structured planning of training phases, loads, intensities, recovery, and competition preparation across days, weeks, months, or a season.

It usually includes:

- macrocycles
- mesocycles
- microcycles
- training phases
- load progression
- recovery periods
- competition preparation
- tapering or peaking strategies

## Why It Matters

Periodization matters because athletes cannot train hard in the same way all year.

Training needs to change depending on:

- the athlete’s goal
- the phase of season
- competition dates
- fatigue level
- injury risk
- training age
- technical needs
- recovery ability

Good periodization helps balance stress, adaptation, recovery, and performance timing.

## Sport Example

In high jump, periodization may move from:

1. General preparation  
2. Specific preparation  
3. Pre-competition  
4. Competition phase  
5. Transition/recovery phase  

During general preparation, the athlete may focus more on general strength, mobility, basic jumping capacity, and technical foundations.

Closer to competition, training becomes more specific, sharper, and lower in unnecessary fatigue.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use periodization to decide:

- what the main focus of the week should be
- when to build strength
- when to build power
- when to focus on technical quality
- when to reduce volume
- when to prioritize recovery
- when to prepare for competition
- how to avoid doing too much at the wrong time

## Decision Rules

- If competition is far away, then training can focus more on building capacity and foundations.
- If competition is close, then training should become more specific and less fatiguing.
- If fatigue is high, then reduce load or adjust the week.
- If the athlete is still technically inconsistent, then do not rush into high-intensity competition-style work too early.
- If performance is improving and recovery is good, then continue the planned progression.
- If pain or injury risk increases, then adjust the plan before forcing progression.

## Common Mistakes

- Training the same way all year.
- Doing too much intensity too early.
- Ignoring recovery weeks.
- Copying elite athlete plans for beginners.
- Planning only on paper and ignoring athlete response.
- Peaking too early or carrying too much fatigue into competition.
- Trying to improve everything at once.
