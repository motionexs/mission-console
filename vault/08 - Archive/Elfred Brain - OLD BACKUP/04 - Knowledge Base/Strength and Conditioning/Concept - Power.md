## Simple Definition

Power is the ability to produce force quickly.

It answers the question:

How fast can the athlete use their strength?

## Technical Definition

Power is commonly understood as force produced over time, or work performed quickly.

In simple coaching terms:

Strength + Speed = Power

An athlete can be strong but not powerful if they cannot use that strength quickly.

## Why It Matters

Power matters because many sports movements happen very fast.

Examples:

- jumping
- sprinting
- throwing
- changing direction
- takeoff actions
- acceleration

In explosive sports, the athlete does not have a long time to produce force. They must produce force quickly.

## Sport Example

In high jump, the takeoff happens very quickly.

The athlete must use speed, strength, stiffness, rhythm, and timing to produce an explosive takeoff.

A powerful athlete can apply force quickly enough to help lift the body over the bar.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use power to think about:

- whether the athlete is explosive enough
- whether gym strength is transferring to sport
- whether the athlete needs more strength, speed, or technical timing
- whether plyometrics, jumps, throws, or Olympic-lift variations may help
- whether fatigue is reducing explosive quality

Power training should be performed with good quality, not just high volume.

## Decision Rules

- If an athlete is strong but slow, then power and speed work may be needed.
- If an athlete is weak, then basic strength may need to improve before advanced power work.
- If power output drops during a session, then reduce volume or increase rest.
- If technique becomes poor during explosive work, then stop or regress the drill.
- If an athlete is close to competition, then power work should be high quality and not create too much fatigue.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking power is only gym exercises.
- Doing explosive work when the athlete is too tired.
- Using too much volume and losing quality.
- Adding advanced plyometrics before the athlete is prepared.
- Ignoring technique and rhythm.
- Confusing strength with power.
