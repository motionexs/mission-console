## Simple Definition

Rate of force development is how quickly an athlete can produce force.

It answers the question:

How fast can the athlete switch their strength on?

## Technical Definition

Rate of force development, often shortened to RFD, describes the speed at which force increases during a movement.

It is important in fast sporting actions because the athlete often has very little time to apply force.

## Why It Matters

RFD matters because many sport actions happen too quickly for the athlete to use all their maximal strength.

In jumping, sprinting, and change of direction, the athlete must produce force in a short time.

So the issue is not only:

How strong is the athlete?

It is also:

How quickly can they use that strength?

## Sport Example

In high jump, the takeoff happens very quickly.

The athlete does not have a long time to push into the ground.

They need to apply force fast while maintaining approach speed, posture, rhythm, and timing.

Good RFD can help the athlete create a sharper and more explosive takeoff.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use RFD to think about:

- whether the athlete is explosive enough
- whether the athlete is strong but slow
- whether plyometrics or fast strength work may help
- whether fatigue is reducing explosive quality
- whether the athlete can apply force quickly during takeoff

RFD training should focus on high-quality, fast, controlled efforts.

## Decision Rules

- If an athlete is strong but not explosive, then RFD and power work may be needed.
- If an athlete is weak, then basic strength may need to improve first.
- If explosive quality drops during a session, then reduce volume or increase rest.
- If an athlete cannot control landings or positions, then use lower-level plyometric progressions first.
- If competition is close, then RFD work should stay sharp, low-volume, and high-quality.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking maximal strength and RFD are the same thing.
- Doing explosive work while tired.
- Using too much volume in plyometric training.
- Progressing to advanced jumps too early.
- Ignoring technique during fast movements.
- Forgetting that RFD must transfer to the sport skill.
