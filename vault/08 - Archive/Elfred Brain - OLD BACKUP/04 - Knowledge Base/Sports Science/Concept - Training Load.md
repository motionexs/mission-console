## Simple Definition

Training load is the total amount of stress placed on an athlete during training.

It answers the question:

How much work did the athlete do, and how hard was it for them?

## Technical Definition

Training load is the combination of external load and internal load.

External load is the work performed.

Examples:

- distance covered
- number of jumps
- weight lifted
- sprint distance
- sets and reps
- training duration

Internal load is how the athlete responds to that work.

Examples:

- fatigue
- heart rate
- soreness
- perceived effort
- mental tiredness
- recovery status

## Why It Matters

Training load matters because it helps the coach balance stress and recovery.

Too little load may not create enough adaptation.

Too much load may increase fatigue, reduce performance, or increase injury risk.

Good training is not just about working hard. It is about giving the athlete the right stress at the right time.

## Sport Example

In high jump, training load could include:

- number of approach runs
- number of takeoffs
- number of full jumps
- plyometric contacts
- sprint volume
- gym volume
- technical session intensity

For example, 20 full high jump attempts may create more stress than 20 low-intensity rhythm runs.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use training load to decide:

- whether to increase training
- whether to reduce volume
- whether the athlete needs recovery
- whether the next session should be technical, intense, or lighter
- whether fatigue is affecting movement quality

## Decision Rules

- If the athlete is performing well and recovering well, then training load may be progressed slowly.
- If the athlete is tired, sore, or technically poor, then training load should be reduced or adjusted.
- If technical quality drops during a session, then reduce volume before bad movement patterns are repeated.
- If an athlete is returning from pain or injury, then training load should increase gradually.
- If competition is close, then training load may need to decrease so the athlete can feel fresh.

## Common Mistakes

- Only counting external work and ignoring how the athlete feels.
- Increasing training load too quickly.
- Thinking more training always means better performance.
- Ignoring technical quality when the athlete is tired.
- Using the same load for every athlete.
- Not tracking jumps, sprints, gym volume, or soreness.
