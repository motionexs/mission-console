## Simple Definition

METs are a way to estimate how much oxygen or energy an activity requires.

It answers the question:

How demanding is this activity compared to rest?

## Technical Definition

MET stands for Metabolic Equivalent.

One MET is the amount of oxygen the body uses at rest.

In the lecture, one MET is described as approximately 3.5 ml/kg/min of oxygen consumption.

An activity with a MET value of 10 requires about 10 times the oxygen needed at rest.

## Why It Matters

METs matter because they help estimate the intensity or energy cost of different activities.

They can be useful when planning general conditioning, health-related exercise, or comparing activities.

METs are not perfect for sport performance, but they give a simple way to understand exercise demand.

## Sport Example

Walking slowly may have a low MET value.

Harder activities like faster cycling, running, or stair stepping require more oxygen and therefore have higher MET values.

In high jump, METs are not a main performance tool, but they can help me understand the energy cost of general conditioning or recovery activities.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use METs to think about:

- how demanding a general activity is
- whether recovery work is truly light
- how conditioning activities compare
- how much oxygen demand an activity may create
- whether an activity is appropriate for the goal of the session

For explosive athletes, METs should not replace sport-specific monitoring, but they can help with general conditioning awareness.

## Decision Rules

- If an activity has a higher MET value, then it generally requires more oxygen and energy.
- If the goal is recovery, then choose lower-demand activities.
- If the goal is general conditioning, then moderate MET activities may be useful.
- If the athlete is already fatigued, avoid adding high-demand conditioning without a clear reason.
- If working with a power athlete, use METs only as a general guide, not the main training decision tool.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking METs explain all sports performance.
- Using METs without considering the athlete’s sport.
- Ignoring fatigue and recovery when choosing conditioning activities.
- Treating all athletes as if the same activity has the same effect.
- Forgetting that movement quality and sport specificity still matter.
- Using general activity values instead of observing the athlete’s actual response.
