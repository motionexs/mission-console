## Simple Definition

Exercise economy is how efficiently an athlete uses energy while moving at a certain speed.

It answers the question:

How much energy does the athlete need to move at a given pace?

## Technical Definition

Exercise economy is the energy cost of activity at a given exercise velocity.

An athlete with better exercise economy uses less oxygen and energy to maintain the same speed or workload.

## Why It Matters

Exercise economy matters because two athletes can have similar fitness levels, but the more economical athlete may perform better.

This athlete uses less energy for the same movement, which can help them delay fatigue and maintain performance longer.

Exercise economy is affected by:

- technique
- body position
- movement efficiency
- stride rhythm
- stride length
- stride frequency
- body mass
- equipment
- sport-specific skill

## Sport Example

In running, an athlete with good exercise economy uses less energy to run at the same speed.

In swimming, better technique and stroke mechanics can reduce energy cost.

In cycling, body position and aerodynamics can affect economy.

In high jump, exercise economy is not measured the same way as endurance sports, but the idea still helps me think about efficient movement. A high jumper should use speed, rhythm, posture, and timing efficiently instead of wasting energy through poor technique.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use exercise economy to think about:

- whether the athlete is wasting energy
- whether movement technique is efficient
- whether rhythm and posture are helping performance
- whether fatigue is causing inefficient movement
- whether the athlete needs technical work instead of more fitness

For high jump, this connects strongly to approach rhythm and technical efficiency.

## Decision Rules

- If an endurance athlete uses too much energy at a given pace, then exercise economy may need improvement.
- If an athlete has good fitness but poor performance, then check movement efficiency and technique.
- If fatigue causes movement to become less efficient, then reduce load or simplify the task.
- If a high jumper loses rhythm and posture, then technical efficiency may be dropping.
- If an athlete becomes more efficient, they may perform the same task with less energy cost.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking endurance performance is only about VO2 max.
- Ignoring technique in endurance sports.
- Ignoring rhythm and posture in movement efficiency.
- Adding more fitness work when the real issue is poor movement quality.
- Forgetting that fatigue can reduce economy.
- Comparing athletes without considering technique and efficiency.
