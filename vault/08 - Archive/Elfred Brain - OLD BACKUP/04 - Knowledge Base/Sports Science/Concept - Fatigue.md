## Simple Definition

Fatigue is a temporary drop in the athlete’s ability to perform because of physical, mental, or emotional stress.

It answers the question:

Is the athlete ready to perform well today?

## Technical Definition

Fatigue is a reduced ability to produce force, maintain movement quality, concentrate, or recover after training or competition.

Fatigue can come from:

- hard training
- too much volume
- poor sleep
- stress
- illness
- poor nutrition
- emotional pressure
- repeated competition
- lack of recovery

## Why It Matters

Fatigue matters because it changes how an athlete moves and performs.

When fatigue is too high, the athlete may:

- jump lower
- sprint slower
- lose rhythm
- make more technical mistakes
- feel heavier or slower
- struggle to concentrate
- have higher injury risk

Fatigue is not always bad. Some fatigue is needed for adaptation. But unmanaged fatigue can reduce performance and increase risk.

## Sport Example

In high jump, fatigue may show up as:

- poor approach rhythm
- slower run-up speed
- weaker takeoff
- lower hip position
- poor posture at takeoff
- bad timing over the bar
- more failed attempts
- reduced confidence

An athlete may still “try hard,” but the movement quality may drop.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use fatigue signs to decide whether to:

- continue the session
- reduce the number of jumps
- change to rhythm work
- lower intensity
- focus on mobility or recovery
- end the session early
- adjust the next training day

The goal is not always to avoid fatigue. The goal is to manage it intelligently.

## Decision Rules

- If fatigue is low and movement quality is good, then continue with the planned session.
- If fatigue is moderate but movement quality is still good, then continue but monitor closely.
- If fatigue is high and technique is getting worse, then reduce volume or change the session goal.
- If fatigue is high and pain is present, then stop high-intensity work and use a safer alternative.
- If fatigue keeps appearing across multiple sessions, then review training load, recovery, sleep, and life stress.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking fatigue is only physical.
- Ignoring mental tiredness.
- Forcing technical work when movement quality is poor.
- Confusing laziness with fatigue.
- Adding more training when the athlete actually needs recovery.
- Waiting for injury before adjusting load.
