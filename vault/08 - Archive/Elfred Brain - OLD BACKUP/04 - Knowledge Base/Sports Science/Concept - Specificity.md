## Simple Definition

Specificity means training should match the demands of the sport or event as closely as possible.

It answers the question:

Does this training actually prepare the athlete for what they need to do in competition?

## Technical Definition

Specificity is the principle that training adaptations are specific to the muscles, movement patterns, energy systems, intensities, and skills used during training.

The aerobic endurance lecture explains that athletes should mimic activities as close as possible to the movement pattern used in competition.  [oai_citation:0‡SCPD 3714 - Learning Unit 7 - Endurance Training - Aerobic Exercise -2024-3.pdf](sediment://file_0000000094f871fd9f9d3ee0e7f912b0)

## Why It Matters

Specificity matters because the body adapts to what it repeatedly does.

If training does not match the sport’s demands, the athlete may improve general fitness but not transfer that improvement well to performance.

For example, a swimmer, runner, cyclist, and high jumper all need different training because their movement patterns and performance demands are different.

## Sport Example

For an endurance runner, running is more specific than cycling because it matches the competition movement pattern.

For a swimmer, swimming technique and stroke mechanics are highly specific.

For a high jumper, specific training includes:

- approach rhythm
- curved approach running
- takeoff mechanics
- sprinting
- jumping
- plyometrics
- power development
- technical timing

General conditioning can help, but it should not replace the most important high jump qualities.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use specificity to decide:

- whether an exercise matches the athlete’s sport
- whether the training method supports the performance goal
- whether conditioning is helping or interfering
- whether the athlete needs general preparation or more sport-specific work
- whether the session is too far away from the actual event demands

## Decision Rules

- If the goal is sport performance, then training should eventually become more specific to the sport.
- If the athlete is in general preparation, then less specific work can be useful to build foundations.
- If competition is close, then training should become more specific and less random.
- If an exercise does not support the athlete’s goal, then remove it or explain why it is included.
- If conditioning interferes with technical or power quality, then adjust the conditioning.
- If the athlete needs endurance performance, then the training mode should match the competition mode as closely as possible.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking all fitness transfers equally to all sports.
- Using random exercises without a clear reason.
- Doing too much general training close to competition.
- Forgetting that movement pattern matters.
- Using endurance training methods for power athletes without adapting them.
- Confusing hard training with useful training.
