# Field Insight - Fatigue Can Disrupt High Jump Rhythm

## Situation

During a high jump session, I noticed that one athlete looked tired.

Instead of improving with more technical jump attempts, their rhythm became worse.

## What Theory Suggested

Technical practice is important for improving high jump performance.

More repetitions can help an athlete improve timing, rhythm, and skill execution.

But this only works well when the athlete can still produce good-quality movement.

Fatigue can reduce coordination, rhythm, timing, and movement quality.

## What Happened In Practice

When the tired athlete was given more technical jump attempts, their rhythm became worse.

The extra jumps did not fix the problem.

When we reduced the number of jumps and focused only on approach rhythm, the athlete’s movement quality improved.

## Lesson Learned

When fatigue is already affecting movement quality, more full technical attempts may make the session worse.

The better coaching choice may be to reduce jump volume and simplify the task.

In this case, focusing only on approach rhythm helped the athlete move better again.

## Possible Decision Rule

If a high jump athlete looks tired and their rhythm gets worse, reduce full jump attempts and focus on approach rhythm before adding more technical jumps.

## Context Notes

This insight is useful for managing technical high jump sessions.

It shows that the coach should not only ask:

“Does the athlete need more practice?”

The coach should also ask:

“Is the athlete fresh enough to benefit from more practice?”

This connects fatigue, training load, recovery, rhythm, and technical quality.
