## Simple Definition

Relative strength is how strong an athlete is compared to their body weight.

It answers the question:

How much force can the athlete produce for their size?

## Technical Definition

Relative strength is strength expressed in relation to body mass.

A simple way to think about it:

Strength ÷ Body weight = Relative strength

For example, two athletes may squat the same weight, but the lighter athlete may have better relative strength because they produce the same force with less body mass.

## Why It Matters

Relative strength matters because many sports require athletes to move their own body quickly and powerfully.

This is especially important in:

- jumping
- sprinting
- change of direction
- gymnastics
- field sports
- combat sports

For jumping events, the athlete must produce high force without carrying unnecessary mass.

## Sport Example

In high jump, the athlete must accelerate, take off, and lift their body over the bar.

A stronger athlete is not always better if they are also much heavier.

The goal is not only to be strong.

The goal is to be strong enough relative to body mass so the athlete can move explosively.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use relative strength to think about:

- whether the athlete needs more strength
- whether the athlete needs more power
- whether body mass is helping or limiting performance
- whether gym strength is transferring to jumping
- whether the athlete can control their own body well

This does not mean forcing athletes to lose weight. It means improving useful strength for performance.

## Decision Rules

- If an athlete is weak for their body size, then basic strength development may improve performance.
- If an athlete is strong but not jumping well, then check power, speed, technique, and rate of force development.
- If an athlete gains body mass, then check whether performance improves, stays the same, or decreases.
- If an athlete has good relative strength but poor jump performance, then the problem may be technical or speed-related.
- If an athlete is a beginner, then improving general strength may strongly improve jumping and sprinting.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking heavier strength numbers always mean better performance.
- Ignoring body mass in jumping sports.
- Focusing only on gym strength without checking sport performance.
- Trying to make every athlete bigger.
- Ignoring movement quality and technique.
- Confusing relative strength with appearance or body shape.
