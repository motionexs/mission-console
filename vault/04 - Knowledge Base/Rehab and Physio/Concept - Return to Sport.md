## Simple Definition

Return to sport is the process of safely helping an athlete move from injury, pain, or limited training back to normal sport participation.

It answers the question:

Is the athlete ready to return to training or competition safely?

## Technical Definition

Return to sport is a gradual decision-making process that considers tissue healing, pain response, strength, mobility, confidence, sport demands, movement quality, and training load tolerance.

It is not just a date on a calendar.

It is based on readiness.

## Why It Matters

Return to sport matters because coming back too early can increase the risk of pain returning or the injury getting worse.

Coming back too slowly can also limit progress, confidence, and performance development.

The goal is to return at the right speed with the right progressions.

## Sport Example

In high jump, return to sport may move from:

1. pain-free basic movement
2. strength and mobility work
3. low-level jumping
4. approach rhythm runs
5. controlled takeoffs
6. partial approach jumps
7. full approach jumps
8. competition preparation

The athlete should not jump straight from pain to full-intensity competition jumps.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use return to sport thinking to decide:

- whether the athlete is ready for impact
- whether jump volume should increase
- whether intensity should increase
- whether the athlete needs more strength or control
- whether pain is acceptable or concerning
- when to refer to a physio or medical professional
- how to progress from simple to sport-specific work

## Decision Rules

- If the athlete has pain during basic movement, then avoid high-intensity sport work.
- If the athlete is pain-free in basic training but not ready for impact, then use strength, mobility, and low-level preparation.
- If the athlete tolerates low-level jumping well, then progress slowly toward more specific drills.
- If pain increases during or after training, then reduce load and review the progression.
- If confidence is low, then progress gradually and rebuild trust in movement.
- If pain is sharp, worsening, or unusual, then stop high-risk work and refer when appropriate.

## Common Mistakes

- Returning based only on time.
- Skipping progressions.
- Going from no jumping to full jumping too quickly.
- Ignoring pain after the session.
- Ignoring athlete confidence.
- Only checking strength and ignoring sport-specific movement.
- Treating return to sport as one decision instead of a process.
