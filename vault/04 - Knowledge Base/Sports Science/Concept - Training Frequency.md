## Simple Definition

Training frequency is how often an athlete trains.

It answers the question:

How many training sessions does the athlete do per day or per week?

## Technical Definition

Training frequency refers to the number of training sessions completed within a specific time period.

In aerobic endurance training, frequency depends on:

- exercise intensity
- exercise duration
- training status
- recovery ability
- phase of season
- sport demands
- athlete goals

## Why It Matters

Training frequency matters because it affects total training load, recovery, and adaptation.

More sessions can create more training stimulus, but they can also increase fatigue if recovery is not managed.

Fewer sessions may be enough to maintain fitness, but more may be needed to develop fitness initially.

## Sport Example

An endurance runner may train many times per week because aerobic endurance is a main performance quality.

A high jumper may need fewer endurance-focused sessions because speed, power, strength, and technical quality are more important.

For high jump, extra conditioning sessions should not interfere with jump technique, sprinting, plyometrics, or strength work.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use training frequency to decide:

- how many sessions the athlete should do per week
- how often high-intensity sessions should appear
- how often recovery sessions should appear
- whether the athlete is training too often
- whether the athlete needs more recovery between hard sessions
- how to structure the training week

## Decision Rules

- If training intensity is high, then frequency may need to be lower to allow recovery.
- If training duration is long, then frequency may need to be lower.
- If the athlete is not recovering between sessions, reduce frequency, intensity, or duration.
- If the athlete is maintaining fitness, fewer sessions may be needed than when building fitness.
- If the athlete is a power athlete, avoid adding too many endurance sessions if they reduce speed, power, or technical quality.
- If the athlete is adapting well and recovering well, frequency can be progressed carefully.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking more sessions always means better training.
- Adding frequency without checking recovery.
- Copying elite endurance training frequency for non-endurance athletes.
- Placing too many hard sessions close together.
- Forgetting that frequency interacts with intensity and duration.
- Using extra conditioning sessions that interfere with main sport performance.
