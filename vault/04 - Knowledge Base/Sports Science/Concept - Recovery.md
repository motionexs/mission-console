## Simple Definition

Recovery is the process where the athlete returns to a better or more ready state after training, competition, or stress.

It answers the question:

Has the athlete recovered enough to train or perform well again?

## Technical Definition

Recovery is the restoration of physical, mental, and emotional readiness after stress.

It can include:

- muscle repair
- nervous system recovery
- energy restoration
- reduced soreness
- better sleep
- improved mood
- restored motivation
- improved movement quality

## Why It Matters

Recovery matters because training only creates the stimulus.

The body adapts after training when recovery is good enough.

Without enough recovery, the athlete may accumulate too much fatigue, perform worse, and increase injury risk.

Good performance is not only about hard training. It is about balancing stress and recovery.

## Sport Example

In high jump, poor recovery may show as:

- slower approach rhythm
- heavy legs
- reduced bounce
- poor takeoff timing
- lower jump height
- more failed attempts
- lower motivation
- stiffness or soreness

A recovered athlete usually moves with better rhythm, speed, sharpness, and confidence.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use recovery information to decide:

- whether to keep the session intense
- whether to reduce volume
- whether to focus on technique
- whether to use mobility or recovery work
- whether to adjust the weekly plan
- whether the athlete is ready for testing or competition

## Decision Rules

- If recovery is good and movement quality is high, then the athlete may be ready for higher-intensity work.
- If recovery is poor but there is no pain, then reduce volume and focus on lower-risk technical work.
- If recovery is poor and pain is present, then avoid high-intensity work and consider referral if needed.
- If recovery is poor for several sessions, then review sleep, nutrition, life stress, and training load.
- If competition is close, then recovery should become a higher priority than adding more training volume.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking recovery only means rest.
- Ignoring sleep and life stress.
- Training hard every day without adjusting load.
- Using recovery methods but not reducing the actual training stress.
- Forgetting that mental stress can affect physical readiness.
- Waiting until performance drops badly before changing the plan.
