## Simple Definition

RPE stands for Rating of Perceived Exertion.

It is a way for the athlete to rate how hard exercise feels.

It answers the question:

How hard does the session feel to the athlete?

## Technical Definition

RPE is a subjective method used to regulate or monitor training intensity.

It is commonly used during aerobic endurance training when direct lab testing, oxygen consumption testing, or lactate testing is not available.

The lecture explains that RPE can be used to regulate intensity, but the relationship between RPE and intensity can be affected by factors like environmental temperature and distractions.

## Why It Matters

RPE matters because it gives the coach information about the athlete’s internal response to training.

Two athletes can do the same external workload, but it may feel different to each athlete.

RPE helps show how hard the session felt from the athlete’s perspective.

This connects strongly to training load, fatigue, and recovery.

## Sport Example

In endurance training, an athlete may rate a run as easy, moderate, hard, or very hard.

In high jump, RPE can be used after a jump session, sprint session, gym session, or conditioning session to understand how demanding the session felt.

For example, a technical session may look easy on paper, but if the athlete gives a high RPE, it may show fatigue, stress, poor recovery, or high mental demand.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use RPE to think about:

- how hard the athlete felt the session was
- whether training intensity matched the plan
- whether the athlete is accumulating fatigue
- whether the session should be adjusted
- whether the athlete is recovering well
- whether internal load is higher than expected

RPE is especially useful when I do not have advanced testing tools.

## Decision Rules

- If RPE is higher than expected, then check fatigue, sleep, soreness, stress, and recovery.
- If RPE is low and movement quality is good, then the athlete may be coping well with the session.
- If RPE is high and movement quality is poor, then reduce load or simplify the session.
- If RPE stays high across multiple sessions, then review the training week.
- If the goal is recovery but RPE is high, then the session may be too intense.
- If two athletes complete the same session but one reports a much higher RPE, then individual adjustment may be needed.

## Common Mistakes

- Ignoring the athlete’s perception of effort.
- Thinking external load tells the full story.
- Treating RPE as perfect without context.
- Forgetting that heat, stress, motivation, and sleep can affect RPE.
- Using RPE without also watching movement quality.
- Asking for RPE but not using it to adjust training.
