## Simple Definition

Training intensity is how hard an athlete is working during a training session.

It answers the question:

How hard is the session for the athlete?

## Technical Definition

Training intensity refers to the effort or workload of exercise.

In aerobic endurance training, intensity can be measured or estimated using:

- percentage of VO2 max
- heart rate
- heart rate reserve
- lactate threshold
- RPE
- METs
- exercise velocity
- pace
- power output

## Why It Matters

Training intensity matters because it strongly affects adaptation and fatigue.

If intensity is too low, the body may not receive enough stimulus to adapt.

If intensity is too high, fatigue may build quickly and the athlete may not complete the session properly.

Intensity must match the goal of the session.

## Sport Example

In endurance training, running at a low intensity can be used for longer aerobic development or recovery.

Running above lactate threshold is harder and usually cannot be sustained as long.

In high jump, intensity can also mean how demanding the session is, such as:

- full approach jumps
- maximal sprinting
- heavy lifting
- high-intensity plyometrics

These sessions create more fatigue and need more recovery.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use training intensity to decide:

- how hard the session should be
- whether the athlete can handle the planned work
- how much recovery is needed
- whether the session should be hard, medium, or light
- whether the goal is adaptation, recovery, or technical quality

## Decision Rules

- If the goal is recovery, then intensity should stay low.
- If the goal is aerobic adaptation, then intensity should match the target system.
- If intensity is high, then duration or volume may need to be lower.
- If intensity is too low, then the session may not create the desired adaptation.
- If intensity is too high, then fatigue may reduce movement quality or end the session early.
- If the athlete is tired, reduce intensity or simplify the session.

## Common Mistakes

- Making every session hard.
- Confusing high effort with good training.
- Ignoring the goal of the session.
- Using intensity that is too high for recovery work.
- Not adjusting intensity when fatigue is high.
- Forgetting that technical quality can drop when intensity is too high.
