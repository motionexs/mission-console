## Simple Definition

Progressive overload means gradually increasing training stress over time so the athlete can adapt and improve.

It answers the question:

How do we make training harder in a safe and useful way?

## Technical Definition

Progressive overload is the planned increase of training stimulus through changes in volume, intensity, frequency, complexity, density, or specificity.

Examples:

- more sets or reps
- heavier weights
- faster sprint speed
- more jumps
- higher technical demand
- shorter rest periods
- more sport-specific work

## Why It Matters

Progressive overload matters because the body needs enough challenge to adapt.

If training is always too easy, progress may stop.

If training increases too quickly, fatigue, poor technique, or injury risk may increase.

The goal is not simply to do more. The goal is to increase training stress at the right speed.

## Sport Example

In high jump, progressive overload could mean:

- gradually increasing approach speed
- increasing jump attempts slowly
- progressing from rhythm runs to takeoffs
- progressing from low-intensity plyometrics to higher-intensity plyometrics
- increasing gym strength loads over time
- increasing technical complexity as the athlete improves

For example, an athlete may start with short approach rhythm runs before progressing to full approach jumps.

## Coaching Application

When coaching, I can use progressive overload to decide:

- what to increase
- when to increase it
- how much to increase it
- when to hold the load steady
- when to reduce load
- whether the athlete is ready for more intensity or complexity

Progress should be based on the athlete’s response, not just the plan on paper.

## Decision Rules

- If the athlete is adapting well and recovering well, then increase load gradually.
- If the athlete is improving but fatigue is rising, then hold the load steady before increasing again.
- If technique becomes worse after increasing load, then reduce complexity, volume, or intensity.
- If pain appears after a load increase, then reduce load and review the progression.
- If the athlete is inexperienced, then overload should be slower and more controlled.

## Common Mistakes

- Increasing too many things at once.
- Increasing volume and intensity at the same time without monitoring.
- Thinking overload always means heavier weights.
- Progressing before the athlete has good technique.
- Ignoring recovery before adding more load.
- Copying another athlete’s progression without individualizing.
